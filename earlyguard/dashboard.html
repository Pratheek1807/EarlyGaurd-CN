Signal Intelligence
EarlyGuard reads 6 raw data sources and compresses months of history into 10 composite signals (each 0–1, higher = more stressed). Those 10 numbers pass through 3 independent ML models, whose outputs are blended by a 4th meta-model into a final default probability — separately for 30 days and 60 days. All feature weights are tunable by the credit risk team without retraining the model.
How 30-day vs 60-day Models Differ
30-Day Model — Acute Stress
Detects accounts already showing payment failure. The training label is went_delinquent_30d — did the account miss a payment within 30 days of the snapshot?

Key drivers: recent partial/missed payments (payment_deterioration_score), recency-weighted payment stress (recency_weighted_stress), and call avoidance (contact_avoidance_score).

The distress is already visible in behaviour — the model is confirming what the data already shows.
60-Day Model — Slow Burn
Detects accounts structurally deteriorating but still paying today. The training label is went_delinquent_60d.

Key drivers: bank surplus shrinking (financial_cushion_trend), income instability (income_reliability_score), and employment events (employment_event_recency).

The account pays now but its foundation is eroding — the model is catching the early warning before behaviour changes.
Composite Score & Label Distribution
Risk tier uses: prob_30d × 0.60 + prob_60d × 0.40. 30-day gets higher weight because it has higher business urgency.

Why These 4 Models — and Not Others
Logistic Regression
Why chosen: Some signals have a near-linear relationship with default — e.g., as debt pressure rises from 0.5 → 2.0, default probability rises almost proportionally. LR captures this cleanly.

Why not alone: Treats each feature independently — misses combinations like high debt pressure AND high contact avoidance together being far worse than either alone.

Why not SVM instead: No native probability output, poor interpretability, hard to explain to regulators.
Random Forest
Why chosen: Builds hundreds of decision trees on random subsets of data (bagging). Naturally detects feature combinations — e.g., self-employed + high income stress + high bureau stress together.

Why not alone: Trees are built independently — doesn't learn from its own prediction errors. XGBoost fixes this.

Why not KNN instead: Computationally expensive at scoring time, no feature importance, sensitive to scale.
XGBoost
Why chosen: Gradient boosting — each new tree specifically corrects the errors of the previous ones. Best-performing model class on tabular data benchmarks. Handles class imbalance via scale_pos_weight.

Why not a neural network: With 10 features and 5,000 accounts, a neural network would severely overfit. XGBoost is the right tool for this data size and type — validated repeatedly in academic and industry benchmarks.
Meta Logistic Regression
Why a 4th model: Each base model has different blind spots. Rather than averaging the 3 probabilities equally, this model learns from training data when to trust which model more — e.g., XGBoost may be more reliable for self-employed, LR for salaried.

Why LR and not another XGBoost: The meta model only has 3 inputs. A complex model on 3 inputs would overfit immediately. LR on 3 inputs simply learns the optimal blend weights — simple and calibrated.

Credit Risk Team — What Can Be Tuned Without Retraining
Feature sub-weights — the 40%/35%/25% splits inside each of the 10 signals are domain decisions, not learned by the model. If a new lender portfolio has different risk drivers (e.g., GST compliance matters more for their MSME book), these weights can be adjusted in the feature pipeline and inference re-run on existing models.
Tier thresholds — Critical ≥ 0.70, High ≥ 0.45, Medium ≥ 0.20 are configurable in the dashboard Parameters panel in real time. A conservative lender may lower Critical to 0.60 to cast a wider intervention net. No retraining needed.
30d/60d blend weight — the 60%/40% split between the two model outputs is a business decision. If a lender cares more about 60-day early warning (e.g., long-cycle recovery), this can shift to 50%/50% or 40%/60%. Model retraining is needed only if the feature formulas themselves are changed.
10 Engineered Signals — 0 = No Stress · 1 = Maximum Stress
Sub-weights inside each signal are tunable by the credit risk team
1 · Payment Deterioration Score
Primary signal · 30d model driver
Last 3 months
Measures how much the account's repayment quality has deteriorated recently. This is the strongest 30-day signal because it directly reflects current payment behaviour with this lender.
Partial payment ratio Weight 40%
Average of payment_amount / emi_amount over last 3 months. Inverted: lower ratio = higher stress.
Why highest weight: paying only 50% of EMI is a continuous signal — it shows both willingness and ability to pay degrading gradually, which correlates strongly with eventual default.
Missed payment count Weight 35%
Number of months with status = "Missed" in last 3, normalised by 3.
Why second: a full miss is a harder event than a partial payment — but partial payments are more frequent and gradual, so partial ratio edges it out.
Average days late Weight 25%
Mean days between actual payment date and due date. Normalised against 30 days.
Why lowest: an account already DPD=0 by constraint, so lateness is mild. It adds nuance but is the weakest pre-default signal of the three.
Score = (1−ratio)×0.40 + clamp(missed/3)×0.35 + clamp(days/30)×0.25
2 · Financial Cushion Trend
Bank statements · 60d model driver
Last 6 months
Captures whether the customer's financial buffer is shrinking. Looks at bank statement trends, not payment behaviour — catches slow-burn cases before they miss a payment.
Net monthly surplus slope Weight 45%
Linear regression slope of net_monthly_surplus over 6 months. Negative slope = cushion eroding.
Why highest: a consistently shrinking surplus is the clearest early warning that the customer will eventually run out of buffer to pay EMIs — even before expenses spike or balance hits zero.
Expense-to-income ratio Weight 30%
Average of last 3 months. Stress contribution begins above 0.5 — when more than half of income is going to expenses.
Why second: snapshot metric — shows current tightness. Less predictive than the slope because a high ratio may be stable if income is also high.
Days balance near zero Weight 25%
Total days across last 3 months where bank balance was near ₹0. Normalised by 30.
Why lowest: an episodic signal — cash crunch days happen even in healthy accounts around EMI cycles. More of a confirming signal than a leading one.
Score = slope_norm×0.45 + expense_stress×0.30 + near_zero×0.25
3 · Income Reliability Score
EPFO + Bank · Employment-type aware
Last 6 months
The only feature with two separate code paths — one for salaried, one for self-employed. A salaried person's income risk looks completely different from a business owner's, so using the same formula for both would lose signal.
Salaried path
Salary delay slope (40%) — is salary arriving later each month? Trend matters more than a single late payment.

EPFO zero-contribution months (35%) — employer not paying EPFO is a strong proxy for job loss or employer distress.

Job change detected (25%) — any job change = income gap risk, even if new job pays more.
Self-Employed path
GST filing status (40%) — Not Filed=1.0, Late Filed=0.5, Filed=0.0. Non-compliance is the strongest business health signal available.

UPI inflow trend (35%) — declining business receipts = falling revenue, the most direct income signal.

ITR filed flag (25%) — not filing income tax = either no taxable income or compliance breakdown.
Salaried score = delay_slope×0.40 + zero_epfo×0.35 + job_change×0.25
Self-Employed score = gst_stress×0.40 + upi_stress×0.35 + itr_miss×0.25
4 · Bureau Stress Score
External credit bureau · Cross-lender view
Latest snapshot
Looks beyond this lender at the customer's full credit behaviour. A customer may be paying us but struggling elsewhere.
Credit score drop vs 6m ago Weight 40%
Normalised by 100 points. A 100-point drop = full stress contribution.
Why highest: the credit score aggregates all credit behaviour across lenders — a drop means something deteriorated externally. It's a summary signal with broad coverage.
Missed payments — other lenders (6m) Weight 35%
Normalised by 5. Customers prioritise secured/larger loans, so this lender is often first to see missed payments.
Why second: direct evidence of default behaviour with other lenders — the most specific signal of financial overextension.
Hard enquiries in last 30 days Weight 25%
Multiple credit applications in 30 days = seeking emergency funds. Normalised by 5.
Why lowest: enquiries alone can be benign (comparison shopping). Valuable as a confirming signal but not a standalone predictor.
Score = drop_norm×0.40 + missed_other×0.35 + hard_enq×0.25
5 · Contact Avoidance Score
Telecall history · Behavioural signal
Last 1 month
Avoidance of collections calls is one of the strongest pre-default signals. Customers who stop answering are typically unable or unwilling to discuss repayment.
Response rate (last 30 days) Weight 35%
Inverted: (1 − response_rate). Not answering calls = high stress.
Why: consistent non-response over 30 days means the customer is either unable to face the conversation (distress) or actively avoiding it (intent). Both correlate with default.
Consecutive unanswered calls Weight 35%
Maximum streak of unanswered calls, normalised by 10. A streak of 10+ = maximum stress.
Why equal weight: a streak is more alarming than an average rate — 10 consecutive ignores is a deliberate pattern, not random unavailability.
Days since last contact Weight 30%
Normalised by 30 days. Silent for a full month = maximum contribution.
Why lowest: long silence may just mean the team hasn't called yet. It's a confirming signal rather than a leading one.
Score = (1−resp)×0.35 + streak×0.35 + silence×0.30
6 · Debt Pressure Ratio
Bank + Loan data · Can exceed 1.0
Last 1 month
Total EMI obligations (this loan + all other lenders) as a fraction of monthly income. The only feature deliberately not clamped to 1.0 — it can reach 2.0 when total EMIs exceed income.
Formula
(this_emi + emi_outflow_other_lenders) / total_monthly_inflow

Uses the most recent bank statement month. If monthly income is zero or missing, ratio defaults to 2.0 (maximum stress).
Interpretation thresholds
DPR < 0.5 — healthy, ample buffer
DPR 0.5–1.0 — stretched but manageable
DPR 1.0–1.2 — EMIs exceed income, unsustainable
DPR > 1.2 → triggers EMI Restructure recommendation
Score = (this_emi + emi_other) / monthly_inflow  | clamped 0–2.0 · defaults to 2.0 if income = 0
7 · PTP Reliability Score
Telecall · Promise-to-Pay tracking
Last 6 months
Tracks whether the customer keeps their word during collections calls. A customer who repeatedly promises to pay and then doesn't is a strong indicator of inability (not just unwillingness) to repay.
If PTP was ever made (last 6 months)
PTP fulfillment rate (50%) — inverted: 1 − fulfillment_rate. Low fulfillment = high stress.

Broken PTP count in last 3m (50%) — normalised by 3. Breaking multiple recent promises = maximum stress.
If no PTP was made
Default score of 0.30 — moderate baseline stress. No contact means uncertainty, not safety.
Score (if PTP made) = (1−fulfillment_rate)×0.50 + clamp(broken/3)×0.50  | Score (no PTP) = 0.30
8 · Sentiment Distress Signal
Telecall · Emotion + tone analysis
Last 1–3 months
Combines the tone and emotional content of collections calls into a single distress score. Drives both intervention tone and offer type.
Sentiment score (last call) Weight 35%
Inverted: 1 − sentiment_score. Low sentiment = high distress.
Dominant emotion (last call) Weight 30%
Cooperative=0.0, Avoidant=0.4, Frustrated=0.6, Distressed=0.7, Hostile=0.8, Resigned=0.9
Hardship mentioned (last 3 calls) Weight 20%
Binary: any hardship mention = 1.0. Also directly triggers "Vulnerability" intervention tone.
Sentiment trend slope (last 3 calls) Weight 15%
Is sentiment improving or declining? Negative slope = increasing distress.
9 · Recency-Weighted Stress
Payment history · Exponential recency weighting
Last 6 months
Similar to Payment Deterioration but uses an exponential recency weighting over 6 months instead of a simple average. A recent partial payment matters much more than one 6 months ago.

10 · Employment Event Recency
EPFO + Bank · 60d model driver
Last 6 months
Captures how recently a significant employment or business event occurred — and how severe it is. More recent events = higher stress. This is a key 60d signal because employment instability takes time to show up in payments.
Salaried path
Job change detected → score = 1 − months_since/6 (more recent = higher).

No job change → checks EPFO zero-contribution months (≥3 → 0.70) or days since last contribution (>60 → 0.50).

No events → 0.0
Self-Employed path
GST turnover trend declining 2+ months → 0.80
Declining 1 month → 0.50
No decline but ITR not filed → 0.40
All clear → 0.0
Salaried score = 1 − months_since_job_change/6  | or 0.70 / 0.50 / 0.0 based on EPFO gaps
Self-Employed score = 0.80 / 0.50 / 0.40 / 0.0 based on GST trend + ITR flag

How Each Recommendation Component is Generated
1 · Action (9 types)
The model finds which of the 10 feature signals is highest for that account — that becomes the top_signal. Each signal maps to exactly one action.
debt_pressure_ratioEMI Relief
employment_event_recencyHardship Call
sentiment_distress_signalEmpathy Outreach
ptp_reliability_scorePTP Re-engagement
+ 5 more mappings
2 · Timing
Pulled directly from best_contact_hour in telecall history — the hour when this specific customer has historically answered.
Salaried
HH:00 – HH+1:00 · 1–2 days post salary credit
Contacts just after salary arrives maximises payment probability.
Self-Employed
HH:00 – HH+1:00 · Mid-month
Business cash flows are typically more stable mid-cycle.
3 · Tone (3 types)
Set by two signals from telecall data: sentiment_score of the last call and whether hardship was mentioned.
Vulnerability
hardship mentioned = true OR sentiment > 0.65 · Acknowledge before any ask
Empathetic
sentiment score 0.40–0.65 · Non-pressuring
Professional
sentiment score < 0.40, no hardship · Factual, solution-oriented
4 · Offer (4 types)
Decided by checking 3 feature thresholds in order — first match wins.
EMI Restructure
debt_pressure_ratio > 1.20 → EMIs exceed income. Reduce EMI 30%, extend tenure.
Payment Holiday
financial_cushion_trend > 0.70 → Cushion almost gone. 1-month deferral, no penalty.
Partial Payment
recency_weighted_stress > 0.75 → Recent stress high. Accept 50% now, rest in 15 days.
Standard Full EMI
None of the above apply. Full EMI by due date.
